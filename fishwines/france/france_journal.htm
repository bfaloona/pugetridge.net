9 October, France "French Countryside" (Barbara)
We're now in Lyon with the Burkes. Seems like so long and so familiar that we have almost settled in here. Familiar, I guess because of being with good friends. The familiarity is good, the ease of just doing things - helping them move, cleaning their new place, cooking meals, laughter over dinner. But after almost a week, we feel somewhat in the way here - in their living room and trying to keep our stuff contained.

11 October … So Paul and I have left Lyon, leaving Annie there with Camille. We took off last night. Got a train to LaClayette (pronounced "LaClet" not "La Clay-et" as the ticket salesman cautioned us). Arrived a little after 7 in the evening and found a somewhat tired hotel where I think we were the only guests. Walked around town - dark, boarded up, empty. A few cafes and boutiques promised more life in the morning. Back to the hotel early, hung out, felt like when we were just dating. Fun.

Oops, forgot the Chateau -quite a nice one in town, surrounded by water - all privately owned. Traffic so very fast, I thought we'd be killed crossing the street at night. Hands shook so much after almost getting hit it was hard to take night photos of the chateau.

This morning after coffee, went to the tourist office - so very helpful. Pointed out the town of Chateauneuf halfway to St. Denis where we are to meet the Burkes two days from now for a weekend with their friends. Tourist office tried to call a B&B for us, but the phone was busy. We started walking the 10 km to the town, heavy mist, slight drizzle. Debated taking the bus. I was leaning that way, (but didn't want to admit it to P. & didn't want to miss the walk). I knew he was leaning the same, but somehow we kept walking till it was too late to turn back. A beautiful walk it turned out to be - rolling French countryside, old farm houses, cows, apple and pear trees by the road (available for picking), easy talking as we walked. Arrived at the B&B by 2pm. The woman from the tourist office had reached the B&B, so our host Madelaine was expecting us. Nice old house, big bedroom, private bath. Home. Things go easy when we open to them - destinations unfold, beds are available, people friendly. And strange things happen. I picked up a magazine in our bedroom because it had an article on Annecy & I'd been thinking of going there. Flipped through the pages trying to refresh my French vocabulary and notice an ad for Twinrix (hepatitis vaccine). I started to say, "hey, Paul, do you remember what Twinrix is?" and halfway through the sentence remembered we were supposed to get our last Twinrix vaccine here in France! There - how did that ad find me?

October 11, La Clayette, France (Paul)
The 10 sure signs that a small French provincial town is dying:

1. The only hotel on the main street is boarded up and has a "for sale" sign on it.
2. They've asphalted all around the church to provide parking for a new commercial development attempting to provide economic stimulus.
3. Everything is dark, shutters are closed and no one is on the street at 8 pm.
4. Cars and trucks passing through town are so unused to pedestrians that you can easily be run over trying to get across the street to the castle.
5. The four remaining young women in town are huddled together looking at their lottery tickets hoping to win their way out.
6. The only evening activity for the young men is to rev their motor scooters and do fishtails down by the river.
7. There are 17 beauty parlors and 5 other specialty boutiques hoping to lure the few tourists that drive through.
8. The owner of the breakfast restaurant has to run around the corner to get bread when you order breakfast.
9. The only evening activity for tourists is to walk down to the steel mill on the outskirts of town wondering what that incredible noise is.
10. All four locals sitting at the bar smoking their breakfast, fix their stare on you as you walk in, pay, and walk out.

Monday, 14 October - fear (Barbara)
We're back in Lyon heading for Strasbourg (Misty's farm).
I hold two images of French life - this vital city, which is a bit somber ("austere",
as the tour book termed it) but still active and urban and the French countryside with its old stone farmhouses and small villages.

In the country/villages, community happens around the kitchen table - one meal fades into the next, wine is poured, dishes cleared and then filled again, talk is the thread that weaves it all together into a day. Time is liquid, melts from hour to hour. You think you have just finished breakfast and someone appears with afternoon liqueurs. Someplace in there a walk is taken, mushrooms picked, jokes shared, problems discussed. Kids appear and retreat like waves on a beach, wash over the table and half the table's food washes back out with them. Somehow, the dishes got washed, the food got cooked, the problems solved. Laughter and yawns punctuate the night, the last plate of cheese, the last glass of wine, bed.

I asked Paul to buy an English paper today without thinking how I would feel reading it. Terrorist bomb in Bali kills 182 people, mostly young vacationers. Young men and women at their prime - happy, dancing. I have traveled these last 2 months with very little fear, meeting people, receiving kindness. I have not imagined one of these terrorists beside me, not grappled with trying to understand their anger and pain. Mine has been a friendly world, these past months. But now I have asked Paul for this fear and unknowingly he has handed it to me.


Friday, October 18th - Malplaquet Farm, La Broque, France "If I were in the mood" (Paul)

If I were in the mood for writing right now, what would I write about? Would it be the cycles of dairy cows or making cheese? How twice a day the ritual begins by walking up to one of the many cow pastures to bring in the cows. Its fall now and the leave are colorful on the rolling hills of eastern France. The air actually feels more like winter and the smell reminds me of snow. Twenty cows, in two facing rows, heads in the feeding trough while the mechanical milkers are attached one cow at a time.

Or would I write about the scene around the dinner table in Bernard's old mansion, with us, Misty, Martine (Misty's mom), Axel and Maxime (Bernard's two boys)? I love being zany with the boys - joking around in the space of the few words that I know in French and the few they know in English: "Hey Man, how's it going?" "Pwiddy Good!" and "AAAAAAAAh fromage nah nah nah nah nah nah nah…" (sung to the tune of "I feel good…. like I knew that I would"). These crazy dinners are like a different world from the one with the cows, just outside and fifty feet away.

Or would I write about Claus who fascinated me. Claus is one of the originals on the farm. He's been there for something like 20 years. I'd put him in his early 50's. To watch him with one of the new dairy cows, squatting down with his head against the side of her belly, eyes closed, calming her to help her get used to this new life of milking with the gentle, deep mocking sound of a cow's voice: this is a man who loves his work and loves his cows. Claus also studied something like ethnology, specializing in the South Pacific Islands and 7 years ago, left the farm to travel for a year, visiting places like Nepal, Cambodia, the Philippines and Thailand while thinking about whether to stay on the farm with it's unique mix of joys and hardship.

Or, if I were in the mood for writing, would I turn my attention inward and think more deeply about myself: How sometimes I fall in love with my thoughts? I think about how others will view what I write and I ride that line between the truth and exaggeration; or the complex feelings I have about looking good and fitting in, in this life on the farm: always working in mud and cowshit or milk processing and dish-washing?

Or would I finally write about community life on the farm? The way people here work together and make decisions, the pros and cons of being such a small group; the relationships between the adults and children or between men and women; and what I might learn from this small, unique community that I might want to bring home with me to our life in Seattle?

October 19 - Malplaquet, France "Cows 1 through 5" (Barbara)
Cows #1:
I have never known cows up close, mostly just passed them driving in a car at high speed through the farmland (me driving, that is, not the cows). Still, for me they're the symbol of the farming life that triggers deep-rooted romantic feelings. Other than that, I know that they have big brown eyes and wet noses. I arrive as a novice on this dairy farm.

Cows #2:
They don't seem to mind being milked (if it is possible at all to read their minds.) They walk in to their milking stalls without complaint - of course, they get fed there. We enter through the center row between symmetrical flanks of gigantic cow heads, locked in position. Huge brown eyes, calm, gentle stares right down the line. 20 in all, 10 on each side. We try to dump a bucket of apple mash (leavings from the cider press) in front of each one as they shove their noses up into the bucket, dumping the contents over their heads. Now that they're occupied with eating, the milking can begin. The milking machines, stainless steel and tubes, such a contrast to their soft, hairy nipples, clash of technologies - god versus man. But still you have to prime the milk by hand and I'm glad there's this soft contact each time. The dangers of milking: #1 shit - it happens & when it does, you better jump out of the way, #2 kicks - a lot more serious than shit, but less frequent. Seems to be a design flaw here - should be a bit more distance between this sloshy brown shit and creamy white milk production. Milking over, its time to head out to pasture again. Here's the trick - rattle the gate before you release their head locks. They always get excited to go out to pasture & when they get excited, they shit (yes, of course) - better to have it in the stalls where it can be washed away than right outside the door mixed with the ankle deep mud you take home soaked into your boots and pant legs. We rattle the gate, they shit. I love knowing things like this, feel like a real dairy farmer.

Cows #3:
The first thing I realize when we approach the herd is that cows are really big. I mean they start to look bigger when you think about interacting with them. If they don't want to do something, it's next to impossible to make them. We are eight people trying to catch one cow who's gone renegade and joined another herd. (How hard could that be?) Klaus sets a "trap" at the bottom of the pasture at the edge of the woods. I can't quite understand the trap, but it seems to have something to do with a bunch of logs stacked up at the bottom of the hill - understanding's not my job anyhow, I'm just here to follow orders. Our strategy is to circle the herd, make a racket and urge the herd forward toward the trap so Klaus can lasso the cow. We all have lassos, but for most of us, it's just for show. I grew up watching westerns, but have no idea how to throw that perfect circle. Klaus admits that he doesn't either and I start to get nervous. We begin slowly: we advance, they move down, we advance more, eight of us with ropes and sticks, the cows retreat toward the trap. It starts to look like this will work. She's close now, the one we want, backed up against the logs of the trap. Klaus wades into the herd, throws his rope, caches one horn, but she bolts (Yes, cows can bolt) heads out toward the edges of our closing circle. The guy she's heading toward yells. I'm just thinking how brave he is when he makes a frantic jump out of her way. She's a fugitive on the run again, back up the hill with Paul sprinting after her. We do this dance for one more hour, chasing her up the hill this time. Now she's through the fence, up the paved road and hiding in the forest (I love that image of a cow hiding in the forest - brings to mind Gary Larson cartoons.). We give up, drag back to the car, a bit sheepish, covered with cowshit and mud to our knees. All the way home, Klaus mutters to himself in German. Later Martin tells us he was very disappointed - third try at catching her and she's still on the loose. She birthed a dead calf seven days ago and is upset. If they don't catch her before her milk dries up, she'll no longer be a milker for this year (that's when we start talking hamburger). Part of me is rooting for this renegade in the woods and part of me would like to see her safe in the barn at the next milking cycle.

Cows #4:
If cows aren't agitated, you can move them easily with a few words (Hey, Oh, Vache), a little prodding. Dogs, well trained, can make them run. Amazing to watch a cow run - an ergonomic incongruity, as if you've just seen your grandmother do a back handspring. The dogs at Malplaquet are well trained (Klaus and Martin argue over whose dog is best - Martin's dog with a slight disadvantage because he was paralyzed and limps strongly on his back leg). The dogs respond to voice commands to move the cows right or left, fast or slow, they'll also hang back on command when a mother is frightened for her calf and stands to challenge the dog. Overall, the dogs do better than we do at moving the cows; I am humbled.

Cows #5:
Dairy farming's a life beside cows. You see them first thing in the morning and last at night. They set the rhythm of your days - in from pasture, feed, milk, out to pasture, process the milk, in from pasture, feed, milk, out to pasture - two cycles a day. And the rhythm of your seasons - moving from pasture to pasture, calving, milking. The scent of soured milk and cowshit huddles around you like a shadow, becomes familiar. I'd like to be here long enough to get to know the herd individually, their personalities, their moods. Klaus talks about the renegade cow like a friend, he's gentle with his understanding of her sadness at the loss of her calf, he worries they won't find her in time before her milk dries up, but he's not too sentimental to know they can't afford to feed her until she calves again next year; he'll walk with her to the slaughterhouse if the time comes. Tough love.

Monday, mid day, October 22, Train to Milano, Italy "Fitting in" (Paul)

Looking good is important to me - or, "fitting in". While on the farm, among a group of hippy farmers, I felt self-consciously conservative looking: Clean-shaven 3 or 4 days ago compared to beards; short hair compared to wild curls and dreadlocks. At Malplaquet, you wake up and start working. Not that people jumped into it so fast, I'm not sure. It's just that: what's the point of cleaning up, showering, putting on clean clothes or combing your hair if you're just going to be covered either in cow-shit, the cheese-making process, engine grease, or some combination of the three?

After 3 days or so, I didn't care anymore about the clothes, pulling on the same stinky clothes everyday. But looking in the mirror, I felt compelled to smooth out my inch-short curls or put on Carl's hat.

Then on Thursday, we went into Strasbourg with Martin to help at the market. Strasbourg is a several hundred year-old city about the size of Seattle an hour from the farm, near the French-German border: Cosmopolitan! Strasbourg had many tourists despite the fact that it was raining and the middle of October. There were beautiful old buildings, cobble-paved pedestrian streets and a space-age-looking tramway. The contrast really struck me as this sleek, quiet, public transit glided between the old stone buildings. I couldn't take my eyes off it.

And along with the well kept, dramatic streetscape were the people: cosmopolitan as well. Well dressed, good looking. Oh, there were beggars and street people but not many. And by and large, people wore black and gray or brown.

I hadn't really thought ahead of time about how people would dress or how the city itself would look. The train ride from Lyon to Strasbourg and on to Schirmeck (the closest stop to the farm) had taken us through a number oaf small towns set into the rolling farmland of this rural countryside. The buildings had changed in style with the German/Bavarian influence and seeing this for the first time from the train, I conjured up no images of people except the stereotypical lederhosen and felt hats.

I did know that I didn't want to wear my cow-shit jeans, thinking: "that might not help sell a lot of cheese." I had two other choices: my dark blue pants that, in retrospect, would have made me more comfortable, and my hand-made, bright red, Guatemalan pants. These, I thought, would be a good choice to keep me warm and they come with good memories of the hardworking, unpretentious people we met in Todos Santos. A maroon fleece jacket, black fleece vest and black short sleeve t-shirt over white long sleeves rounded out my ensemble. I also brought a red maroon raincoat and Carl's hat for the inevitable rain.

After a great conversation on the ride in the van with Barbara and Martin, we arrived at the market outside the center of the city, set up the cheese cart, parked the van, served a few customers and went for coffee. After a few minutes, Martin left to tend the cheese cart while Barbara and I were instantly transformed back into tourists with instructions on how to get to the cathedral and an area of Strasbourg with well-preserved 500 year old buildings, tourist info/souvenir shops, upscale hotels and trendy boutiques - "Petit France" it's called.

It's amazing to me how I feel in these circumstances. That, how I'm dressed relative to where I'm standing, determines whether I fit in or feel out of place. Why is that? Sure it's a symptom of insecurity. In my mind, the "right" thing is to feel good about myself, "who I am" regardless of where I am or how I'm dressed, etc. "I am me:" similar in some ways and different in others from every other human being. No better, no worse, just similar and different. That's what this trip is about - exploring these similarities and differences and seeing how people form community in light of these similarities and differences and observing also how these are expressed in culture and community.

So, I'm thinking that there are two ways that I feel comfortable - either I have a place, a role, or am convinced that I'm accepted and, even better, loved by those around me, or I have the proper "look" which allows me to disappear from the view of others. Funny thing. It feels like the tape inside goes: "fit in and disappear until you find yourself in a position to be loved and accepted by others. Then, work as hard as you need to to earn that love and acceptance." For me this work includes being smart, being funny, being a good boy. Sometimes I think I'm handsome too which also helps me "earn" acceptance, but as I get older, I feel this less often.

I can't help believing that most of us share this tape: that we need to earn our place of love and acceptance and that doing so requires pretty constant vigilance. Luckily, there are lots of consumer products available to help.

Being a tourist is rough in this light. I posses neither the clothes/looks to fit in, knowledge of the local game, nor time to "work" my way in. So, this makes me resistant in the first place. Small towns and longer stays have helped, allowing me to meet people and "get to work." It doesn't actually feel like work though, as the feeling of acceptance, of camaraderie and friendship - especially in a short time across cultural differences - is really rewarding. I love it. In Todos Santos, El Bosque, and Malplaquet: there's been a small enough community and a long enough time to get to know people, share on a deeper level, see our similarities and differences and accept each others' friendship on a remarkably deep level.

I'm proud of this, enjoy traveling this way and want to do more. But, I also want to push at the edges of my insecurity. I want to feel comfortable as an outsider in a place that's not my own, to wear my Todos Santos pants without being self-conscious, and to accept myself without having to work for acceptance.

Afternoon, October 24 - Loro Ciuffena (Annie)

Today was a good day. Not great, not bad. We went to Camicia and took a bus to Cortona. We walked to a castle and skipped down the twisted roads. I saw lots of cats and met a really mean one - he kept pushing the other cat away. When we came home on the bus, we left the backpack on one of the busses. It had the digital camera in it. Afternoon, October 26 - Loro Ciuffena About a book we read called Shabanu (Annie)

I am soooo lucky. I think forced marriages are bad. In the book they got married at my age. I couldn't do it. I'd run away. The thought of having sex any time sends shivers down my spine. I wonder if, in any country where you prepare all your life, it would be easier. I don't think I could stand to live like that. It seems disgusting. Tears are in my eyes, I don't know why. Maybe I feel sorry for her. Maybe just the thought makes me miserable. For some reason, I didn't really like the book. Maybe it is our culture. Maybe it makes me feel bad about growing up.

26 October 2002 - my first big loss (Barbara)

The first big loss of the trip has happened - just material things, but still I wake up with a hollow feeling, fantasizing their return (somehow I confront the thief, scare him and get the things back.) What wears on me is that my camera is gone - my art, my touch on this world that we travel through. And I see how much I depend on it, smile through its lens. A sweet part of the trip that I grieve now it is gone. I feel foolish; it is a small thing in the scale of things, but a joyful way that I connect with the world and thus sad in its loss.

We are with Ed and Sharla now and they have been so kind through this all - giving us time to deal with trying to find the pack (which we had actually left on a bus from which it was stolen) and then file the paperwork for its theft. We're all in Loro - a sweet small Italian hill town with houses leaning on each other, climbing up the hill like pilgrims crowding into a temple at the top. I'm not as settled here as in Spain and France. Have not connected much with the people also, though some have been kind in helping us try to find the bag and fill out paperwork. Can't say I've given it much of a chance - absorbed in myself. I can see that this morning I am finding myself again and this writing is just a way to do it - each word a drop of water settling into a hollow until a small pool collects and the pool is me.

The colors here in Tuscany are gold and green - golden buildings, green landscape, green shutters, gray-green olive trees - a subtle landscape. Green, gray, gold, sprinkled with red flowers, geraniums mostly at this time of year. I suppose I haven't seen it in its glory - under the sun and clear blue sky, nor with the warmth of my heart, being preoccupied as I was. Perhaps today will be different.

I have not thought much about Annie much these past few days also. Camille is with us and their energy is a small bonfire in the midst of us -we feel its warmth, but it seems self-sufficient and it's rare for us to feed it a log. They like to be read to and we read Shabanu - a story of a young Pakistani woman, written by a woman in NYC and I wonder how much she really knows about Pakistan. But we all cry at the end when the spirited young woman succumbs to a life with someone she does not love, vowing to hide her inner heart from him forever.

9 November 2002 - Paestum, Italy (Barbara)
We are now in Paestum Italy with Ed and Sharla, but I want to capture here my time alone in Rome.

I arrived on a train with P & AJ. I disembarked and they went on to Sorrento to meet up with Ed and Sharla. After jumping down to the platform, I felt so alone and stupid. Walking the length of the train, I wanted so much to just get back on & go on to Sorrento with them. I imagined the look on their faces as they saw me reappear. But I plugged on ahead and stopped at the first phone to call and try to find a place to stay. First I tried the UW Rome office to see if someone I might know was there & they might just have a place for me to stay. But no luck - they were all out on a field trip. So I reluctantly pulled out the Servas list. The 3 Servas families I had emailed hadn't responded and I felt very uncomfortable calling this late & asking for a room that night. I decided to try one I hadn't emailed (they only had a phone number listed and I am still sheepish about using the phone for international calls!) The phone rang once & was picked up by a woman who didn't speak much English. She turned the phone over to another woman. I was trying to figure out how to politely phrase that I was a Servas traveler & need a place to stay that night, when she gushed out "Are you from Servas?" "Yes." "From where?" "the US." "Do you want to stay here?" That was it, I had a bed for the night. Thirty minutes later, I was in the home of Angelina & Giovanni & wrapped in the energy of Irene, their daughter. Irene, it ends up, was just visiting to go to the hospital with her father that night. He was scheduled for bypass surgery the next day. They fed me, introduced me to a family friend, Angelo, (who gave me a person to contact in Ethiopia) & then proceeded to drive us all to the hospital to admit Giovanni. We finished up with a quick night tour of Rome. At the end of it, Angelo headed off to a theology class and Irene grabbed me by the hand to catch a bus back home in time for her train out that night! (Irene lives in northern Italy - "Come to visit there any time" she says, "It's nicer than here.") Whew!

From the lonely train platform of 4 hours earlier, the world had flipped. I was enveloped in this family, sharing their wine, stories, jokes, disappointments, joys and many noodles! Angelina (the angle of peace) showed me photos of her peace marches, Irene's wedding, her own wedding, Servas guests from the past. Angelina dotes on me and her two small doge - her new babies since her kids have left home. With Giovanni and Irene gone, Angelina wraps me into her life - good food, political TV shows, old sentimental movies, yapping dogs & everyday chores. During the day, I tour Rome and the UW program, then come home each night to Angelina & the dogs and it does feel like a home to me, after such a short time. Irene calls, Angelo calls, Giovanni calls (from the hospital where his operation has been delayed) and I talk to them all on the phone. I am part of their home for a brief few days and I see the true intentions of Servas at, I think, its best - not a shiny polished best, but a warm and human best.

I get a sense for Rome while I am there. A sense of the history that crisscrosses this city and literally collides with its modern facades. The power of the church, the struggles for control and grandeur. It wasn't a major focus for me on this trip, but this history of western civilization is creeping into my consciousness by the sheer weight of its presence.

I am also glad to visit the UW program. Good to see old friends, both professors and students. Re-ignited the desire I have to teach, to be a part of the UW scene. I love the world of architecture and ideas, pictures and drawings - such a luxury to be able to work among it.

And lastly, in Rome, I replaced our camera. I knew in my heart that someplace in Rome the Nikon 990 was sitting on a shelf, waiting for me - if I could find it, if I didn't give up. So I stopped in every camera store I found and on the last day, on my way back home, I poked my head into another store - a little hole in the wall. No English, so I wrote down "Nikon 990". The shopkeeper said "No", then hesitated and said "Usata?" (used?) I said "Yes, used OK!" He took me to the window and there it was, sitting on the shelf in perfect condition. It's mine now & I am grounded in my photos again!

Tuesday afternoon, November 12 - Bus to Piraeus, Greece (Paul)

I don't want to forget Europe. And for some reason, now, I'm afraid that I will. I was not enamored with Europe. It seemed ordinary, familiar. And now as we enter Greece, our first stop with a different alphabet, and head toward Turkey, it feels like I'm leaving a good friend at the dock and watching my friend become smaller and smaller as the boat goes off and away. I remember so much about Guatemala: the people we met, the landscape, places we stayed, events we participated in. It seems like we were there last week. In Europe, though, we moved around a lot more and traveled differently, much of the time like regular tourists: sticking together with friends and family, to English, to the guide-book inspired tourists routes. So, we interacted with people whose job it is to interact with tourists: hotel and restaurant staff - like that.

Despite all that, we had some wonderful times with friends and family and saw some amazing places. The things I want to remember of Europe:

  • The old part of Sevilla - exciting, organic city.

  • Close-knit Spanish families! Together at lunch time, afternoon naps and late evening strolls for dinner - especially inspiring in the Triana neighborhood of Sevilla

  • The beautiful young women of Spain

  • So many motor scooters buzzing all over the streets of Spain

  • Being sick in Triana with an ear infection and a fever for several days, visiting the neighborhood clinic, being treated well and almost fainting in the waiting room

  • Being appalled by the crap I saw on TV in Spain

  • Getting lost daily trying to find our way to Spanish school in Sevilla, once drenched by a downpour that closed local schools

  • Seeing a few short minutes of a great Flamenco performance in a packed neighborhood bar in Triana that started past midnight

  • Great, Spanish busses - cheap, new, clean, and always on time

  • The white-washed hill towns of Andalusia - clean, community-friendly places with a mix of uses, extended families, public squares, and life on the street late into the night

  • Friendly, engaging people - especially the men - of Andalusia, Pedro in El Bosque, "the guys" at Villamartin

  • Horse dancing and flamenco dress in Villamartin

  • Watching Annie go out with the girls - new friends her age - past midnight, in El Bosque Spain

  • Our tedious day in the plaza in El Bosque and our local friend counting in English

  • Terraced hillsides with olive trees and wine vineyards throughout Spain, France and Italy

  • Walking into Grazalema at sunrise, taking an intense hike along the edge of a steep rocky mountain and coming upon a natural arch and a precipitous drop overlooking this beautiful Spanish hill town

  • Meat! The only food in Spain

  • Making up a new card game ("Strategic War") on the steps of a statue outside the Alcazar in Sevilla while killing time before our train to Barcelona

  • Wonderful Gaudi buildings in Barcelona - but most of these national treasures could only be experienced by paying an entrance fee or being wealthy

  • A room in a hostel with a balcony overlooking the action-packed "Las Ramblas" in Barcelona

  • Lyon, the perfect scale for a big city - 8 story buildings, great transit (buses, subways, surface tramways), everything you need within walking distance. Not a tourist site, just a great city

  • The fantastic view of Lyon from the balcony of the Burke's apartment

  • Old, pedestrian cities on rivers throughout Europe. Built to last and still gracefully housing urban life 300-700 years later, usually surrounded by car-oriented urban sprawl where the car is clearly winning, the nicest parts overtaken by upper class boutiques, junk food and too much fashion!

  • An amazing group of people at Malplaquet farm, living/working together, running a farm, caring for cows, goats and pigs, making cheese to sell at local farmers' markets. The hard, constant work, the beauty of the land, the personalities and just an inkling of how they all fit together. The feeling of both receiving the gift of being able to experience this magical place and contributing to the work of the farm while we were there.

  • The wild juxtaposition of the centuries old downtown of Strasbourg, France and the space-age tramway running through it.

  • Fresh apples and pears picked off the tree walking from town-to-town through the French country-side

  • The seeming importance of fashion among young people everywhere we were in Europe

  • Witnessing what felt like a dying small town in rural France, wondering if this is a trend as TV, the internet and pop music peddle their ubiquitous images of what's "in" ("plastic, sexed-up, I'm cooler than you and I don't care") to young people who can't reconcile this attractive image of what they want with the 700 year old small town heritage around them

  • A weekend of some of the best (and most!) food, wine, laughter and friendship with long-time friends of Veronique and Rod in rural France

  • How fantastic, central and prevalent the churches are all across Europe, where the line between religion and tourism is very blurry

  • Seeing Sienna, Italy from the inside and from above, the whole city an incredible architectural and sculptural museum. Il Campo, with it's most impressive municipal building taking on a stature that rivaled the church; climbing to the top of its tower and seeing how farm and forest tumbled away from the walls enclosing this dense medieval city

  • The dramatic setting of the medieval town of Loro Ciuffena with its steep-gorge waterfall along the edge of town and the view out our window to the clock and bell tower

  • Laughing hysterically with my parents as my dad added dialogue to the unfolding scene around us, predicted the acts of strangers and mastered the Italian language

  • Spending 30 hours trying to recover the backpack (with raincoats and digital camera) that I left on the bus - to no avail

  • The 8-story town of RioMaggiore on the Cinque Terra coast of northern Italy clinging to the rocky cliffs for 700 years and sleeping 5 flights up with the windows open, listening to the surf

  • Sitting out on the rocks watching the crash and spray of the Mediterranean one windy night with Barbara, Annie, Rod, Veronique and Julien (Camille had fallen asleep)

  • Floating on the Mediterranean, flat out, with my outstretched toes and hands out of the water. For the first time in my life not sinking!

  • Going out through the crashing waves on the rocky beach to ride the swells before the impending storm

  • Writing and sharing haiku poetry with the Burkes in RioMaggiore before our last night together, touched by how much we could share of our love for each other in 17 syllables

  • Barbara and I joining another couple balancing rocks on the beach

  • Montevarchi, a working class Tuscan city, with no special tourist appeal but that I fell in love with for the dozens of people in their 70's and older who rode their old-style bikes around town; the great card game with Annie and Camille in the town square (having missed our bus to Sienna); and the church tower with a pedestrian walkway through it and a bell tower that chimed the half hour and the hour - bell visible as it swung back and forth

  • Pizza, pizza, and more pizza throughout Italy

  • The deteriorating and a little more than frequently late public transit in Italy and tons of graffiti

  • The unbelievable bus ride along the Amalfi coast with its tightly clustered villages perched clinging on the rocky cliffs above the blue Mediterranean, a dozen diving dolphins way below, crazy car and motorcycle drivers passing us on blind curves

  • Being struck by the amazing similarities of ours and the daily life we could imagine walking around Pompeii, the Roman city buried by volcanic ash and mud 2000 years ago: streets with curbs; mixed use zoning; one-way, two-way and pedestrian only streets; take-out restaurants on street corners; public baths, theaters, stadiums and markets - all amazingly preserved

  • A wonderful weekend with my folks, Nicholas and Guido at a water buffalo farm near Paestum on the west coast of Southern Italy: amazing fresh local food prepared and served gourmet style, the sweet and pompous idiosyncratic ways of this middle-aged couple of Belgians; seeing 300 + horned, black, dairy water buffalo and the process of making fresh mozzarella (good for only 3 days); walking through the 2500 year old Greek temple ruins of Paestum - marveling at how they could have been built; great pizza nearby and cracking up hysterically at my dad's imitation of Nicholas

  • Spending five days sleeping together with my folks in a single room in Sorrento, Italy at the Hotel Desiree with amazing people who took great care of us
