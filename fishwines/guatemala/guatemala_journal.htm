Note: The following journal updates from Paul, Barbara and Annie were received on Friday, Sep. 6. They were going to leave Guatemala the next day and spend a day in New York City before flying to Spain on the evening of Monday the 9th. Click to the left to view the pictures, with captions.


Wednesday night – August 6th – Antigua,  Annie

 I still can’t believe that we are on this trip.  I miss my house already.  It’s just weird we were on a plane all day and I still don’t get it.

I am sort of embarrassed to say “Hola!”  I think it might start a conversation I can’t finish.  I’m afraid my Spanish isn’t good enough.

There are so many people on the street it’s depressing, too many to count.  I wish I could just get them all a house.

I miss my house.

Saturday night – August 10th – Todos Santos Cuchumatan, Paul

It’s really cold here in Todos Santos but we’ve got this really cushy room.  Tile floor, nice beds, a small table with 2 chairs.  It’s really wild though.  Outside our door is another world.  Todos Santos is an overgrown 3rd world village.  There’s this main street exactly 2 busses wide between sidewalks, add 5 feet total for the sidewalks on each side to the face of the buildings.  We arrived today at about 4 pm.  Market day was over but there were still lots of people.  Lots of people.  2 other busses arrived within 10 minutes of ours – Todosanteros getting on and off the busses plus the usual shops and street vendors.

The last hour of this bus ride through very steep, gorgeous farmland/mountains, was on a dirt road – very bumpy, muddy (it was raining – no windshield wipers) and very scary.  Major drop off!  We’d seen signs elsewhere in Guatemala that said “Peligroso” (dangerous) but they didn’t even bother with this road that dead-ends in Todos Santos.  It’s the most remote place that I’ve ever been.  Remote in the sense of what it takes physically to get here.  The Internet hasn’t reached here yet but there are a few cars and pick-ups, electricity (which also means music and TV) and other signs of the 21st century.

But Todos Santos is clearly very poor.  Most people here are Mayans and wear typical clothes unique to this village.  The women - deep blue skirts and colorful shirts, the men - red striped pants and denim-like shirts with wide colorful collars.  Many also wear small-brimmed straw hats with Mayan color bands but many have traded them in for baseball-type caps (SF 49ers, Nike…).  Even children and teens dress this way and age doesn’t seem to be the factor of why people choose which type of hat.

What makes this community hang on to this tradition?  Do they talk about it?  I’d love to find out.  And what’s the relationship between the uniformity of dress and the sense of community?

Back in Huehuetenango you can see the Todosanteros around and know where they’re from – other village dress being different.

This morning in Huehue, we gave Maria a “tip” which really touched her (she’s a Mayan women – housekeeper/cook for the Ladino Gonzales family – Servas Hosts we stayed with) and walked around town trying to find the Internet café Annie and I were at yesterday.  We gave up and found another.  Barbara was typing away while Annie and I sat on a bench in the lobby.

I looked over at Annie and noticed she was looking sad.  After a few wrong guesses on my part as to why and a little coaxing, Annie told me that every time she tried to start a conversation I ignored her, didn’t laugh at her jokes, etc.  “I’m sorry Annie, you’re right.  I’m feeling kind of nervous about being here and haven’t gotten used to traveling yet.  I’m worried about speaking Spanish and thinking that I should be asking directions about the bus today.  Thanks for point it out to me kiddo, I’ll try to do better.”

The fact is, there’s something going on with me about feeling out of place: feeling like I don’t belong here and wondering how a whole year of this is going to feel.  Walking up to people and speaking Spanish is a challenge for me.  I’m taking it on pretty well but it really puts me up against that feeling of being out of place – an “invasive species.”

Maybe there’s a connection between these feelings and the benefits of all of “one’s people” wearing “red pants”.  Maybe it’s one of the reasons that there are fashion trends.

With our packs on, wandering around El Centro of Huehuetenango, we got as many answers to our quest for bus directions as people we asked.  All at once, a bus was pulling up to the corner on which we were standing that was headed for the bus terminal.  We scrambled up the ladder with our packs on and into the emergency exit at the back of this converted school bus.  There was plenty of room.  Annie initiated a game of hand slapping – her reminder to me earlier about being present helped me as we amused several of our Guatemalans fellow bus riders with our game.

In typical fashion so far on this trip, our switch to the Todos Santos bus was totally smooth and off we went.  As the ride went on, the bus become more and more empty until there were only about 10 of us getting off in El Centro de Todos Santos.We even managed to get set up with our host family and our cushy room.  Once off the bus, we greeted by a couple of boys asking if we wanted a hotel.  They seemed about 7 years old (later found out that they were 11!).  There are three language schools in Todos Santos and we didn’t know which one we were in.  We only knew that the director’s name was Nicole.  The kids seemed very confident that they knew Nicole and which school this was so we followed them back across town to Proyecto Linguistico.  The director was not in town but after a pretty short wait and some soup across the street, Margarito, one of the instructors set up with Flori, our host.  Orientation tomorrow (Sunday) and Spanish language school starts Monday at 2 pm.

Our host family is huge – grandma, several of her sons and daughters and their kids.  One of the daughters is 13.  She works at home in the mornings and goes to school from 2-6:30.  I asked if Annie could come with her to school sometime and she said yes.  Annie said she’d like to but not right away.  She wants to get to know her a bit first.

This house is a crazy labyrinth of habitable, barely habitable and uninhabited spaces that I haven’t figured out yet.  I’m really looking forward to getting to know our family and instructors and getting a feel for the life in this overgrown village.


Monday evening – August 12th – Todos Santos, Paul

Too much Spanish packed into this brain!  The first day of classes de Espanol – 5 hours, one-on-one.  At the end, we covered “Cuanto Cuesta?” – how much does it cost?  Jorge asked me “Cuanto Cuesta una taza de café?”  (a cup of coffee).  I said Seattle is famous for special coffee and people can easily spend $3 on a cup of coffee.  Then $200 for a fancy hotel room.  The I asked, “Cuanto gana un maestro de esta escuela?”  How much does a teacher at this school earn?  Then we were comparing the cost and standard of living of our two countries.  It was the end of the lesson but the most interesting part for me.  Jorge stopped being the teacher and we were engaged in our exploration.  We ended with a shared understanding that there’s really no difference in “what it takes” to live and that the money part is not important.

This morning before lunch, I spent 3 hours on a construction site with Elbi, the brother of our host Flori, who is building a big house a few doors down.  Many differences from job sites at home.  There were piles of sand and gravel, scrap wood and concrete block, puddles of water with rebar and other scraps floating.  One difference was, that there were 3 or 4 kids playing in the puddles while we worked.  Ages 2 ½ to 10.  Elbi and 2 masons were up on top of the 2nd floor just hanging out, looking down.  Santos and I had stopped sifting sand from the gravel pile and were also just standing there.  Elbi yelled down to ask the kids how deep the water was.  Santos had picked up Maria, the 2-½ year old and was saying, “look up at your Poppy up there!”  Elbi tossed down some bills and told Santos to take Maria around the corner to buy her some boots so she could play in the puddle too.  Off we went, Santos and I, each of us holding one of her hands – an important part of building this house!

About 15 minutes after we returned, pan y frescos (bread and soda) was purchased for the crew and we took a “break”.  Maria sat in Elbi’s lap and he re-did the barrette in her hair.  At another point, a friend stopped by.  He as drunk, wearing typical Todos Santos clothes with an “NHL” patch on the arm.  He spoke some English and was clearly bothered by me but was playing around.  He brought Elbi and I a beer and told us to drink (we did) and told me the fucking tourists run the Guatemalan economy.  With a wink from Elbi and Santos, I figured things could get weird if I didn’t drink the beer and keep nodding my head.  Eventually he left.  Mothers came for their children, other kids came and played with the sand as we were sifting, etc.

I don’t know how much block was laid, but in 3 hours, we had managed to mix a few yards of cement, sift a few wheelbarrows of sand, and give several kids some loving adult attention.  As far as I could tell this was the standard for industrial production here.

I really admired and enjoyed being with Elbi and Santos.  I liked how they treated me, the other workers, the kids, and the house they were building.  None seemed more important than the other.  I have no idea about the economics of this venture, so it’s tough to see the whole picture but assuming it’s at least marginally harmonious, I really appreciate the balance.


Saturday/Market Day – August 17th – Todos Santos, Paul

5:32 am           I roll over and check my watch.  The morning is quiet except for the rooster.  The sun, not yet risen, lights the sky.  The ever-present smell of smoke is in the air as is the morning chill, although it’s warm enough for shorts.  At 5:37, the multiple toots of the bus leaving for or arrive from Huehuetenango.  At 5:42 another.

The house is dark and quiet as I leave, the street we live on, empty.  As I turn onto the main street, the first bus passes.  Dozens of Todosanteros line the street, buy coffee standing, while the “Perro callejeros” (street dogs) fight for scraps.  The bus “captain” is unloading firewood and other baggage from the top of the bus and loading it again while people board.  All of this in time with the slow drone of the musica blaring from the Iglesia (church) across from the central plaza.

The cobblestone main street is the repository of everything here in Todos Santos:  fruit peels, garbage, the jokes of young people, buckets of wash water, kid’s laughter, men’s pee, market transactions, drunken men sleeping off the trespasses of the previous night, Mayan women walking with baskets of squash and other fruits of their labor, babies strapped to their backs.

6:00 am           Another bus takes off.  Busses leaving Todos Santos are virtually empty while those arriving are packed, three to a seat and standing the aisles.  The busses have names here, like “Flor de Maria”.  They’re not just machines, but trusted companions over the remote and often dangerously steep mountain roads.  The sky is now bright enough to have turned off the streetlights.  There’s now other music competing with the church as trucks unload more goods for the market.  Strolling through the church plaza and the main market street, my throat is pretty raw from a week of wood smoke, diesel fumes, dust from the streets and the two construction sites I’ve been periodically helping on.  Tarps are being spread out and makeshift tables set up.   Only one woman is ready to sell:  oranges, peaches, green beans, etc.

Why am I here watching to Todos Santos Market come alive?   Why am I on this trip?  I certainly wouldn’t trade what I’ve got for life here.  Is it to make me appreciate the life I have at home?  While they don’t have paved streets, central heating and the environmental regulations we have, families work together here, socialize, and eat together in a graceful, fluid manor.  Children help unpack and sell veges, while parents give them caring attention at the same time.  Standing in line for a cup of coffee is a social affair for those in line and for those serving.  A group of 10-11 year old shoeshine boys joke and laugh, and shine each other’s shoes between the few customers they all share.

7:15 am           The sun just came up over the Cuchumatan Mountains east of the valley and it feels good.  Lots of people are here now, from the busloads and truckloads that have arrived over the past 2 hours.  The market’s mostly together but still moving slowly.  Busses and trucks clog the narrow street shared by venders, shoppers, and others carting boxes, bags, bins, etc.

There are no cell phones as of yet in Todos Santos and no Internet service and the pace of life reflects that less instantaneous time when to speak with a relative in another pueblo still means a half day walk or 2 hour bus ride.  A Toyota pick up truck takes off with 15 men, women and children in the back.

8:30 am           Tourists, alone or in groups, stop at a distance in the middle of the street, not used to the grit, and hesitate to make mistakes, not knowing exactly where or how to go about this.  This market is not for them.  In fact, I’m not sure who it’s for as there’s a lot of walking around, socializing and hefting but not many transactions taking place.  It seems just a larger version of what’s here on any other day:  fruits and veges, indigenous and “regular” clothes, food, cassette tapes and CD’s, plastic junk, cook wear, watches and handmade jewelry – a big flea market.  Lots of people selling the same thing too – people from the surrounding mountain pueblos taking the 2, 3, 5 hour bus or truck ride here to sell their produce alongside Todosanteros.  “Why this market is here” or “who it’s for” seem not like legitimate questions.  “It’s what we do.” I imagine the answer to be, “It’s what we’ve always done.”


August 18, 2002, Barbara

It’s a good day – unfolding slowly, pleasantly.  P. left for a trip up the mountains with some other men here (to play a soccer game).  I took a walk early before he left – to get some exercise, as Annie hasn’t been too excited about hiking here.  Came back & had coffee on the porch with P. before he left.  Annie woke up and we went to Las Ruines (the local ruins) with Magdali & her 2 cousins.  Played Frisbee, hide & seek, & I drew and studied Spanish.  Boys playing at the ruins wanted to draw, so I gave them the sketchbook.  Others started playing with the second Frisbee, so I gave them that & was happy to know the word for gift.  Sketched a donkey & photo’d the girls beside it.  Back home for lunch, then went to buy some candles with Annie.  Am in the mood for candles.  Something softer in me is present today.  Have just finished playing Crazy 8’s with the girls and was in need of being by myself a bit so now I’m writing.  Annie is happily with them today. 

The beads I brought have worked out well.  The girls & Estrela enjoyed making jewelry together last night – a nice gift without the commercialism.  We have thought well about what to bring on this trip (thanks also to Bob for his gifts of pencils and markers for the local school!)

Money passes around here from neighbor to neighbor.  So many people sell one thing or another, so many little tiendas (stores).  Living like this, we could spend almost no other money (other than what we have paid the school) & part of me is tempted to do that, but part of me also feels a drive to support the local economy.  How Flori’s household makes money:Flori prepares meals for the local school (850 kids) 4 days a week and everyone in the household works on that.Flori & Amparo bake breads and sell them locally (I’m not sure where yet, some people seem to come to the door when they smell the baking)Flori rents out 2 rooms in the house for the language school.Two brothers have a construction company, building houses.  Another brother is a teacher in training & probably isn’t making money right now. 

This is a place of simple life.  Home and work overlap and flow together.  Family connections are strong.  There is fluidity to life here; roles don’t seem to be so strict.  It is hard to tell who the children “belong to”. Only when a mother is carrying a baby is it obvious, but the generations are also very broad so that it took me a week to realize that Magdali (13) is actually Flori’s sister, not her daughter. A swirl & flow of children among adults.  It is frequent to see children with grandparents, walking together, frequent to see toddlers with teenagers on the street.

Sounds are wonderfully full here – one of the differences from home.  My walks are often accompanied by music bursting from someone’s home & it gives me that strange feeling of being in a movie.  This morning was no different and I opened my senses to the sound.  I moved from a romantic love song to the chunking industrial sound of some processing of corn meal which I don’t fully understand yet.  That gave way to the more rhythmic clacketing of manual looms – quieter & not so synchronized so their rhythm formed a jerky dance.  Later on, the chanting from the churches, not the church’s favored Latin, but a haunting Mam (the local indigenous people) chant that we her almost daily.  It makes me wonder who is really in control of these churches. 


Tuesday morning -  August 20th - Todos Santos, Paul

It’s been a few days since I’ve journaled.  I’d say we’re much more in the swing of things here and enjoying ourselves.  It’s 10:30 am and Barbara, Annie and I are sitting on the patio, the sun shining, no smoke for a change, Maria, Otto and Heremias (the 3-5 year olds) laughing and playing circles around us. 

We just got back from the school.  We visited a couple of classes today in the main school.  853 kids, 40-70 in a class with one teacher.  Marguarito teaches in our language school as well as at this school.  His class was really fun.  He had a great/fun control over his class.  The kids sang to us, Barbara drew a map on the whiteboard and we talked about where Guatemala was, the US, our home, etc.  We also sat through a bit of Marguarito’s lesson and couldn’t quite follow it.  Something about the animals, the vitamins foods contain, etc.  This was 2nd grade, kids were 10-11 years old.

Last night we lost power.  There were about 7 or so people watching TV, I think this family having a TV is rare here.  When the power went out, Annie floated the idea that we take the candles and cards to the other room to see who wanted to play.  We lit two candles, brought them to the kitchen and started a great game of “ochos locos” (crazy 8’s) – easy to teach and play.  Annie and I, Polo, Magdali, and Elbi played with several others watching and the little ones on the floor, walking around or on laps.  We played a number of games for about an hour I guess.  Time stood still as we had simple fun in the kitchen under candlelight with the whole family.  It was nice to have the mesmerizing influence of the TV gone.  It’s not on that often but the other night, while cutting veges for school lunch (Flori has the job of preparing hot lunches for the 850 kids at school) the TV was on – worse than US TV.  The main themes were women’s breasts and a series of people’s pants falling down.  There were other channels but this was the top choice.

This past Sunday, I was invited to go with Elbi on an excursion for the day.  I understood that it was with his soccer team, up into the mountains but I didn’t understand that they had a game with the tiny Aldea (smaller than a village) of Shichim.  15 of us, ages 15-40 (plus or minus) got into a Toyota pick up, 12 in back, and headed out of Todos Santos.  The scenery was spectacular climbing out of the valley into the highest part of Central America.  I couldn’t follow half of the conversation but there was a ton of storytelling and laughter the whole way up.  I’m guessing it was a 2+ hour drive.  It seemed the older guys were the main storytellers but mostly everyone contributed – except for me and perhaps 2 or three others.

Along the way, the rocky, high Cuchumatan Mountains stretched out in the clouds, a bit of rain fell on us and it got pretty cold.  We passed several small clusters of Mayan houses made of wood, concrete block, and stone, with thatch, tin or tile roofs along the way, and arrived in Shichim – a group of buildings that made Todos Santos feel like the big city.

I followed along as we wandered around trying to find someone, without luck, who could prepare potatoes for the 15 of us after the game.  Finally, a local man volunteered to figure this out for us by the time the game was over.

The field was really rough as it was covered with rocks and dished about 4 feet to the center.  It was cold and a really strong wind blew lengthwise across the field.  It was a big surprise to me when everyone began pulling out uniforms and suiting up for “the game.”  I felt a bit in a funny spot – wanting to play but feeling like I should be invited if that was appropriate.  The team looked to have a pretty wide range of skill and that I would probably fit in o.k.

When everyone was about changed, someone said to me: “Come on, you’d better change.”  “I don’t have a uniform.”  “O.K., maybe the second half.”

It was really fun both to watch and play.  I felt very much part of the gang (“Vamos Pablo!).  During the first half, I would fetch balls that the wind or a stray kick sent away from the field.  4 or 5 young boys came over to check me out.  It looked like they had never seen a gringo before and were both excited and scared by being near me.

Playing was pretty difficult.  First, I’m not a soccer player, but the wind, and field conditions contributed.  The toughest part though was the altitude.  At 11,000 feet, the thin air really affected my ability to run.  I did manage to get into a few plays, got 2 shots off in the direction of the goal and of course, we won 7 to 4.  One guy got 6 of the 7 goals.  At one point, he went up to head the ball and crashed into a player on the other team who kept running.  He went down and was bleeding from his mouth and nose and I would have guessed it to be pretty serious.  Several Todosanteros yelled for water but no one from Shichim moved.  At least half the town was there (the men) watching.  I noticed a group of women with blue blankets draped over their shoulders going into or coming out of a small stone church across the road from the field at about mid-field.  I wasn’t sure if this lack of help was about team or village loyalty or what.  It didn’t seem to me that there was any tension or super-competitive vibe.  In any care, he got up, spit out the blood, blew his nose and continued playing without any fanfare.

After the game, we changed, piled into the truck and drove the few hundred yards back into the main part (10 or so buildings) of the Aldea to find our lunch spot.  The place that had been arranged was a little room in back of a concrete block building with 2 tables in a small room with a bare light bulb, ready to receive us with the food we had brought warmed up (tortillas and what seemed like beef) and the potatoes and tea they had prepared for us.  Compared to the truck ride, it was pretty quiet as we say and ate, tired and hungry.  Rain fell outside and we cracked open the bottle Aguardiente (“las vitaminas”), the Guatemalan version of rotgut alcohol.  Mixed with the orange juice I had brought, it worked o.k.  Only a few guys drank but not to excess and many didn’t drink at all.  There wasn’t any arm-twisting or expectations about it and the whole experience of the day struck me as a very un-“macho” one.  The guys were a very friendly, fun-loving, close-knit bunch, comfortable with arms around each other, sitting on laps, etc.  They were brothers, cousins, co-workers, teammates, friends.

After lunch, we piled back into the truck and headed out of the town - the other way - down this steep set of switchbacks.  If not for the constant jostling along the dirt road, there was little evidence that we were on the road as, looking over the side, you looked several hundred feet down over the edge.  We drove on for a while through this incredibly remote high country: steep, forested cliffs and rock outcroppings.  Ahead was a think cloudbank, which filled the valley, mixing with the trees and cliffs.  It was spectacular as we drove on into the clouds.  Lone houses with Barefoot Mayan families chopping wood and weaving textiles dotted the landscape every mile or so.  Wow, this is a remote place.

The truck stopped along the road and we all got out to explore the site of a gringo’s house (“Samuel”), which was burned down in the guerilla violence of the 1980’s.  The house, they said, was beautiful and apparently Samuel was a great benefactor of the surrounding villages.  A couple of the guys knew all about it and gave a great oration with sweeping gestures.  The house site was spectacular, on the edge of a steep cliff that must have been at least several hundred feet straight down.  Most of the vista was shrouded in clouds.  Not being able to see what was out or down there added to the mystery of the place.  At one point, one of the guys called us over to the edge to point out a circular rainbow straight down the cliff into the clouds as a ray of sunlight penetrated from above.  Wow (again!).

One of the guys brought a camera and we took a couple of “team photos” one with and one without me and we were off. 

On the way home, it rained a bit and the aguardiente was poured.  We toasted the team but I’d say 4 guys, not including me, did most of the drinking.  We got home around 5:30.  Dropping down into the valley of Todos Santos, we dropped off a couple of guys on the edge of town and pulled onto the main street to drop off the rest of us.  Polo and I walked up the street home while Elbi stayed with a few others to return at about 8 pm or so staggering drunk.  Polo was on one arm and Flori on the other, helping him safely to bed to end a great Sunday.


21 August 2002 – Power and Water, Barbara

We have been without water and power for much of these last couple days – it’s hard to tell why no one here seems much concerned.  The water is off most days for at least several hours in the afternoon, or all afternoon – that seems predictable. We’ve also lost power the last 2 nights & I was happy that I had bought candles & that our flashlights are working. 


25 August 2002 – Weaving, Barbara

Our time here is starting to feel like it has a rhythm – warm ritual of familiarity.  Weekdays, classes are from 2 pm – 7, so we have the mornings free.  Time for hiking, writing, reading, weaving or whatever.  That & classes pretty much fills the day. 

Evenings are low key.  The evening meal with the family is at 8.  It’s still a bit awkward for me – my Spanish isn’t quite up to it and we’re waited on more than I’m comfortable with.  We eat in the kitchen which is relatively small – about 10’x15’, including the wood stove.  But the fire here is vented with a chimney to the outside – something I’ve come to appreciate after being in other kitchens where the smoke just rises to the top of the ceiling & out the gap between the walls & the roof.  The kitchen table is small & only seats 4, so that’s reserved for P, Annie, me & Cornelius (the other American staying here).  Other members of the family sit around the walls or next to the stove where it’s warmer (I usually try for the chair nearest the stove).  We’ve tried to invite others to the table, but usually it doesn’t work.  After dinner we hang out & talk for a while.  It’s best when we can get some joking going & Cornelio is usually good for that – he loves to be the butt of the jokes.  

Late nights is low key fishwine family time in our room – our funny fishwine combination of playing cards & reading to each other. 

Weaving has added a new rhythm.  Last Wednesday, Annie & I went to have weaving lessons from Marcela, an 18 year old Mam woman. First she pulled out a bag of colors & told us each to pick 5.  Annie had been thinking a lot about it & quickly picked out a range of greens & blues & one white.  I hadn’t thought at all about it & was dazzled by all the colors.  I sorted through them and came up with a red, a purple, two blues and a green.  Marcela tried to get us both to choose a yellow, but we both declined & I realized how conservative we are in colors, even when we’re trying to push ourselves into a new culture. 

Then she took us to buy the string/yarn (more like embroidery thread, really).   We came back to her house to wind it into balls.  After the first hour, I started thinking this was all a big mistake; it would take forever to do anything.  I had to regroup or give up.  So I convinced Annie to let me help her wind her yarn & get her started weaving – then I’d think about mine.  It took us both 3 hours to wind hers – there we were at the end of 3 hours of work with 5 little balls of yarn – about the size of a hardball.   Annie said “I bet in the US you could buy this already in a ball.” I smiled & nodded – end of weaving day #1. 

(A bit about Marcela, She lives in a one room, dirt floor house surrounded by a cornfield & flowers.  It’s an idyllic spot for us, but tight for them (her mom & sister).  We weave on the covered area adjacent to her house.  P. tried to take pictures of the house & corn, but it’s all too close.  Marcela is beautiful – a combination of the natural beauty of the Mam people & a warm friendly spirit.  Her “personal space” boundaries are far different from the American norm (at least for women).  She holds & hugs & strokes us all the time, even patted my breast on the second day to ask how long I had nursed Annie.  Then played with my hair and asked me why some of it was white.  She loves Annie & I love to see them working together. )

Weaving day #2
This morning was spent stringing the colors for the warp strings on a 6’ long board with pegs in it.  Marcela showed Annie how to weave the strings through the pegs, a rhythmic dance with both hands.  So wonderful to see Annie pick up the rhythm of the movement – a whole body movement.  Six passes with each color & then on to the next, and so on through all the colors, then start again.  For another 2 hours. End of day  #2.

Weaving day #3
Marcela soaked the strings in cornstarch & stretched them on two sticks which would end up being the end two pieces of the loom.  They dried all morning but Paul, Magdali, Annie and I returned in the afternoon so Marcela could show Annie how to set up the rest of the loom.  Two more hours, stringing, tying – intricate, complicated work, and the weaving hasn’t started yet.  We left Annie at Marcela’s, just as the weaving started.  She’s comfortable there, but still brave – a new country & Marcela doesn’t speak any English.  I’m learning so much respect for this daughter of mine on this trip. 

Weaving day #4
Weaving continues & Annie’s cloth is about 6” long.  The weaving goes much faster at this point than I had expected, but I am still astounded at the amount of work it takes. 

So I think about Marcela and the other weavers of this valley.  A very intricate weaving (much more intricate than Annie’s first one) will sell here for about $10-12.  It will use about $4 worth of materials and take at least 10 hours of work.  And yet in front of every other house there is a woman weaving – weaving fabric for the huipiles they wear, weaving for the market - a beautiful, rich craft, but how long will it survive like this?

.Our bus passed him,
With his hat and scarf and staff
On a mountain road.
He looked up
With that same expression.
My eyes met his
And we held the moment
Of the passing bus.

And the women,
Them too, I saw.
Walking in a line beside the fields
In all their purples and reds and greens,
Children on their backs,
By their sides.

The men in the truck
Coming back from the crops or the market
Yes, crowded into the dark green pickup.
Purple shirts, straw hatsLike in the magazine.
And they are laughing.The bus too, our bus
Red with stripes of yellow and purple
Gold fringe at the window
A young man hanging out the door
Front or back
Or scrambling up the ladder
To fling luggage on top
Strong arms, warm eyes.

The rest of us
Crowded three or four to a seat
Paul, Annie, me,
The Mam and Ladinos
Passing bread and photos and stories around
Laughing at our broken Spanish.

Just the chickens are missing -
The ones in the crate on the farmer’s lap.
I wonder now
Where that National Geographic photographer
Found that crate of chickens. 

Here in Todos Santos, we’re closer to nature

The steep, tall Cuchumatan Mountains that surround this pueblo
Forested valleys and cultivated slopes
Yielding corn, potatoes, squash and melon
The morning sun fuels their growth, dries the corn, our clothes
Half the “house” is outside
Fresh air mixing with wood smoke
Between our bedroom, the bath, the kitchen

Here in Todos Santos, we’re closer to wet.
The August rains that show up after lunch
Filling the creeks
Watering the corn
Making the steep, stone street dangerously slippery for walking
Basins of water
Always filled or filling
To wash and cook
And for times when water service stops
Sometimes for days

Here in Todos Santos, we’re closer to cold
Afternoon, when the clouds descend the mountain valley
Bringing their chill
The house has no heat
Except for the cook stove in the cocina
Extra blankets on our beds

Here in Todos Santos, we’re closer to death
A funeral procession through the streets yesterday
Four bodies in white coffins on shoulders
Guitars and criers wailing
Following them to “El Parque Central” and the cemetery
A small girl fell 15 feet through the railing at El Parque
And just the night before,
A drunk man 2 stories from the roof
Of an unfinished second floor

Back home,
Central heat, building codes, hand rails and health insurance
Keep us warm and dry and safe
In the illusion that we are guaranteed a long, clean life.


Friday morning – September 6th – Antigua, Guatemala, Annie

The end.

Can’t believe it.  It seems like we’ve been gone for like a week, but a whole month!  First I thought it would be helpful for me to make friends and I would become more open but I hate saying goodbye.  It almost makes me not want to make friends.  Then, I wouldn’t have anyone to miss.  It’s a weird feeling.  I want to be done with this place so I can be one place closer to home but also, I want to stay longer to get to know people better.  Like in Todos Santos.  No one has a phone or computer so I would need to write to keep in touch.  Do you know how much it would cost to have a pen pal in Guatemala and how long it would take to get letters to and from them?

It’s amazing how people drive here.  To get to some places, you have to drive on roads that are just big enough for 2 trucks to fit with about a foot to spare and the steep hillside.

People drove like it was any old road and all you have to worry about was not to cross the yellow line.  On the day we left Lanquin, a truck went half way off the side and we were stuck for about an hour or so.  The bus was cautious there but when it got past it was like nothing ever happened.  I noticed people here are really open to share their friendship, their love, their world, house, culture…it’s really sweet.

In Todos Santos, I met a 16 year old named Marcela.  She taught me how to weave.  She lived in a one-room house with her mom and sister who was 14.  She loved me.  She seemed to think I was so beautiful.  It’s funny, I never thought so.  And every time I came, she would say, “Annie!” and run up and hug me saying to anyone there, “A mi me gusta ella!” (“I like her!”)  When I left, I decided I wanted to give her a heart of beach glass from the coast near my house.  When I gave it to her, she said that whenever she looked at it she would think of me.  We hugged, I cried and I left.  I hate saying goodbye!

Soon we will be in Spain and I realize that I miss certain luxuries like big towels and dishwashers and washing machines.  Bit I feel now I could live without some I never thought about.  It is good not to get stuck in thinking you need all that stuff.  It’s amazing how much we have in the U.S
